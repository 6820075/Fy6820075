Abstract

Many new methods of harvesting electrical energy have been discovered. Triboelectric nanogenerators could be the small, cheap solution to find sustainable replacements to power sources that harm the worlds environment. Triboelectric nanogenerators have the capability to be used in many areas, for example in clothes in soles of shoes and on human joints. This article explains the process of the creation of electric energy and how much energy a Triboelectric nanogenerator can produce. It also explores, how human activity could easily play such a big role in creating green energy.

Main Article

Triboelectric nanogenerators could be the solution to harvesting sustainable electrical energy from human activity. Some of the most powerful versions of the triboelectric nanogenerators that have been made are so small they can sit between two fingers. This size allows the triboelectric nanogenerators to fit and work in almost any situation you could imagine. Alongside their nano size they are also relatively low cost to manufacture and have a high efficiency conversion rate. The process used by triboelectric nanogenerators is that of transforming a mechanical force into electrical energy. The question is how can you harvest the mechanical energy required? One way is to use the movement of different areas of the human body. As the joints in the human body contract and move apart, it creates a mechanical movement that the triboelectronic nanogenerators can harvest and produce electrical energy from (Nasimul Alam S,2023). Different locations in the human body can produce small amounts of power in watts, as shown in Fig 1, Fig 2 shows the comparison of what energy different electronic devices need to be powered. Another process is the compression of two materials. As materials compress, they create a frictional force between them and when decompressed that frictional force creates and flow of electrons within the two materials. For example, sometimes when a person takes a jumper off and it rubs over their hair, they may recieve a small electric shock. That flow of electrons created from the decompression of frictional force can be harvested by the triboelectric nanogenerators and turned into electrical energy. Many people track their steps for health benefits so why not also track your steps producing electrical energy at the same time. Triboelectric nanogenerators could be placed into the soles of running shoes, the compression process of the foot pressing against the floor on every step would produce friction. This is an example of the mechanical energy needed for the transfer to electrical energy in the nanogenerator. The triboelectric nanogenerators could also be incorporated into clothing used for exercise and other human activities. The tyoes of fabrics that come into contact with the body under mechanical force and hence produce the electrical energy are made of common textile materials such as polyester, cotton and wool. These materials are cheap and can be sewn into different clothes, for example socks.
